üéØ What Are Nuclear Bodies?

Nuclear bodies are membrane-less organelles that form through liquid-liquid phase separation, creating distinct compartments within the nucleus. These dynamic structures concentrate specific proteins and RNAs to facilitate various cellular processes, from ribosome biogenesis to RNA processing and gene regulation. Understanding their biophysical properties is crucial for comprehending nuclear organization and cellular function.

üî¨ Key Biophysical Concepts

Phase Separation

The thermodynamic process by which nuclear bodies form through liquid-liquid demixing, concentrating specific biomolecules while excluding others.

Molecular Condensates

Biomolecular assemblies that behave like liquid droplets, exhibiting dynamic exchange with the surrounding nucleoplasm.

Multivalent Interactions

Weak, multiple binding interactions between proteins and RNAs that drive phase separation and maintain nuclear body integrity.

Critical Concentration

The minimum concentration threshold required for phase separation and nuclear body formation.

Viscoelastic Properties

The material properties of nuclear bodies that determine their fluidity, fusion behavior, and response to cellular forces.

Compositional Control

Mechanisms that regulate which molecules partition into nuclear bodies and which are excluded from these compartments.
